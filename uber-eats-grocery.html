Redesigning Uber Eats Grocery for Item-Led Planning

How I would reduce basket-building friction in a store-bound delivery model using AI-assisted planning.

Type
Concept case study
Focus
Grocery · Item-led planning
AI
OCR · NLP · Store-fit scoring
Discipline
Product Strategy · UX
Overview

Make grocery planning item-first without breaking store-bound fulfillment.

Problem: Users must pick a store before knowing whether it can fulfill their full grocery list, forcing premature commitment.

Solution: Add an item-first planning layer that parses a grocery list, compares store fit, and recommends the best starting basket.

AI role: Multimodal input parsing, list understanding, store-fit scoring, substitution handling, and basket validation.

PM focus: Reduce large-basket friction, improve basket completeness, and increase conversion without changing the underlying store-bound operations model.

Introduction

As a frequent user of grocery delivery services, I noticed a recurring friction in my own shopping workflow. I then approached it as a PM: framing the problem, understanding the operational constraints, and designing a solution for Uber Eats Grocery.

Uber Eats Grocery currently requires users to select a store first, then build a basket item by item within that store's catalog. There is no efficient way to input multiple items at once, and no mechanism to identify the best-fit store based on a full intended list before committing to one.

That being said, the store-first flow exists for valid operational reasons. Uber's logistics model relies on a single driver picking up from a single location, and real-time inventory, pricing, and delivery ETA can only be pulled accurately once a store is selected.

I wouldn't try to remove that store-bound operational model. Instead, I'd redesign the planning layer before store commitment, so users can start with what they naturally know, their intended items, while still preserving the operational model that makes reliable delivery possible.

The Problem

Turn an intended list into a complete, accurate cart, with confidence nothing was missed.

Uber Eats Grocery creates friction in two connected ways.

1 · Store selection too early

Users begin with item-based intent: they know what they need before they know where to get it. But the flow requires store selection first, before they can tell which store best fits their full list. That forces an early commitment without enough information, when the real goal is to complete a list, not choose a store.

2 · Basket building too manual

Once inside a store, users reconstruct their intent within that store's catalog. Every item needs its own search, selection, and add flow. The longer the list, the worse it gets, so the highest-intent users, those doing a full weekly run, are penalized most.

Business Impact

Two ways this drives impact.

1. Recover high-intent grocery sessions currently lost to input friction.

2. Improve basket completeness and reduce missed-item regret before checkout.

I'd expect the biggest near-term upside from friction reduction in large-basket sessions, with basket validation becoming more valuable as matching quality improves.

Solution Design

An item-first planning layer ahead of store commitment.

Four steps: multimodal input, list understanding, and store-fit recommendation form the core, with a context-aware completeness check as a Phase 2 layer once the core flow is trusted.

01
Multimodal Item Input

Let users start where their shopping intent already exists, in text, notes, photos, or screenshots, instead of rebuilding it inside the app.

Input typeAI processing
Text file / notes / pasteNLP parsing → item extraction
Photo (handwritten) / screenshotsOCR + vision model → text → same pipeline
02
AI-Powered List Understanding

Parse and normalize raw input into structured grocery items by resolving ambiguity, standardizing quantities, and mapping to catalog-recognizable terms.

Designing for imperfect inputs matters as much as the happy path. A user who writes "big eggs" or "Heinz" still expects the right item, so this layer surfaces low-confidence matches for review rather than silently committing them.

03
Store-Fit Recommendation

Surface the best-fit store on four criteria, before the user commits:

  • Item coverage — what % of the list does this store carry?
  • Estimated total cost — matched item prices plus delivery fee.
  • Delivery ETA — within an acceptable range.
  • Substitution confidence — how reliably the partner handles out-of-stock items.

Item coverage is likely the strongest factor, since users are trying to complete their list. But coverage alone isn't enough, and the recommendation needs to be clearly explainable to build trust. Parsed results appear in a review screen before anything is committed to cart, so users can correct mismatches first.

04
Context-Aware Completeness Check Phase 2

After store selection and review, AI adds one final confidence layer before checkout. When the basket strongly implies a specific meal, the system surfaces a tightly scoped completeness check to help users catch likely-missing essentials, not to drive discovery or basket expansion.

"Your cart suggests taco ingredients. Want to review likely missing essentials like lettuce or salsa?"

Once this layer is trusted, Uber Eats could later explore broader surfaces such as meal inspiration or recipe discovery that go beyond the user's original intent.

MVP Scoping

Ship the core flow first, layer completeness later.

The single-store model stays, because it's still the most operationally sound foundation. The opportunity is to reduce pre-cart friction by helping users find the best store for their list, not to change fulfillment. So the MVP makes input, list understanding, and store-fit work reliably first.

What I'd Validate Next

Three assumptions I'd pressure-test with internal data first.

1 · Drop-off by item count. Does abandonment actually increase with list length? If the curve is flat, the friction opportunity is smaller than estimated.

2 · Partner catalog coverage. What % of typical grocery lists can a single partner fulfill? Low coverage degrades store-fit quality and may need a different UX treatment.

3 · Completeness inference accuracy. How reliably can the system infer a meal from basket contents? If inference is weak, Step 4 triggers too rarely or surfaces irrelevant suggestions.
