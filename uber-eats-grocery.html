Uber Eats · Concept Case Study

Redesigning Uber Eats Grocery
for Item-Led Planning

How I would reduce basket-building friction in a store-bound delivery model using AI-assisted planning

Product Strategy AI/ML Grocery UX Design
Introduction The Problem Business Impact Solution Design MVP Scoping Decision Success Metrics What I'd Validate Next
Introduction

As a frequent user of grocery delivery services, I noticed a recurring friction in my own shopping workflow. I then approached it as a PM: framing the problem, understanding the operational constraints, and designing a solution for Uber Eats Grocery.

Uber Eats Grocery currently requires users to select a store first, then build a basket item by item within that store's catalog. There is no efficient way to input multiple items at once, and no mechanism to identify the best-fit store based on a full intended list before committing to one.

That being said, the store-first flow exists for valid operational reasons. Uber's logistics model relies on a single driver picking up from a single location, and real-time inventory, pricing, and delivery ETA can only be pulled accurately once a store is selected.

I wouldn’t try to remove that store-bound operational model. Instead, I’d redesign the planning layer before store commitment, so users can start with what they naturally know — their intended items — while still preserving the operational model that makes reliable delivery possible.

The Problem

The job to be done is to help users turn their intended grocery list into a complete, accurate, purchasable cart with confidence that nothing was missed.

Uber Eats Grocery creates friction in two connected ways:

1. Store selection happens too early

Users often begin grocery shopping with an item-based intent: they know what they need before they know where to get it. But Uber Eats Grocery requires them to begin with store selection, before they can tell which store best fits their full list.

This forces users to make an early commitment without enough information, even though their real goal is to complete a list, not choose a store.

2. Basket building is too manual

Once inside a store, users must reconstruct their shopping intent within that store’s catalog. Every item requires its own search, selection, and add flow, making basket creation slow and repetitive.

This input friction not only makes the experience feel inconvenient, but also directly hurts cart completion and conversion. The longer the list, the worse the experience. Users with the highest purchase intent, such as those doing a full weekly grocery run, are penalized most.

Business Impact

This concept could drive business impact in two ways:

1. Recover high-intent grocery sessions currently lost to input friction

2. Improve basket completeness and reduce missed-item regret before checkout

I would expect the biggest near-term upside to come from friction reduction in large-basket sessions, with basket validation becoming more valuable as matching quality improves

Solution Design

Step 1 — Multimodal Item Input

Let users start where their shopping intent already exists, in text, notes, photos, or screenshots, instead of forcing them to rebuild it inside the app.

Input TypeAI Processing
Text file / notes / pasteNLP parsing → item extraction
Photo (handwritten) / Screenshots OCR + vision model → text → same pipeline

Step 2 — AI-Powered List Understanding

Parse and normalize raw input into structured grocery items by resolving ambiguity, standardizing quantities, and mapping to catalog-recognizable terms.

Designing for imperfect or ambiguous inputs is as important as the happy path. A user who writes "big eggs" or "Heinz" still expects the right item in their cart, so this layer needs to surface low-confidence matched items for user review rather than silently committing them.

Step 3 — Store-Fit Recommendation

Surface the best-fit store option based on four criteria, before the user commits to a store:

  • Item coverage — What % of the list does this store carry?
  • Estimated total cost — Matched item prices + delivery fee
  • Delivery ETA — Estimated time to delivery should be within acceptable range
  • Substitution confidence — How reliably does this partner handle unavailable items for the substitutes when exact items are out of stock?

Item coverage should likely be the strongest decision factor, since users are primarily trying to complete their intended list. But coverage alone is not enough. Total cost, delivery ETA, and substitution confidence also shape whether a store is truly the best fit, and the recommendation needs to be clearly explainable to users in order to build trust.

AI matching can be imperfect. Parsed results should be surfaced in a review screen before anything is committed to cart, so that users can correct mismatches before proceeding. The core design principle is to use AI to accelerate intent completion while keeping users in control before anything is added.

Once user selects the store, any remaining gaps, such as unavailable items, are surfaced for user review.

Step 4 — Context-Aware Completeness Check

After store selection and review of matched items, AI can provide one final confidence layer before checkout. In cases where the basket strongly implies a specific meal or shopping mission, the system can surface a context-aware completeness check to help users review likely missing essentials.

This is a tightly scoped completeness check designed to help users catch ingredients they likely intended to buy, not to drive discovery or basket expansion.

Sample Messages:

“Your cart suggests taco ingredients. Want to review likely missing essentials like lettuce or salsa?”

"This basket looks like a pasta dinner. Want to review likely missing essentials like parmesan or garlic?"

Once this confidence layer is trusted, Uber Eats could later explore broader recommendation surfaces, such as meal inspiration, recipe discovery, or add-on suggestions that go beyond the user’s original intent.

MVP Scoping Decision

The single-store model remains in place because it is still the most operationally sound foundation for grocery delivery. Given these constraints, the product opportunity is to reduce pre-cart friction by helping users identify the best store for their intended list, rather than changing the fulfillment model entirely.

And then, the MVP should focus on making item input, list understanding, and store-fit recommendation work reliably first, then layer in a context-aware completeness check once the core flow is trusted.

What I'd Validate Next

Before building, I would pressure-test three assumptions with internal data:

  1. Drop-off by item count: Does abandonment actually increase with list length? If the curve is flat, the friction opportunity is smaller than estimated.
  2. Partner catalog coverage: What % of typical grocery lists can a single partner fulfill? Low coverage degrades store-fit recommendation quality and may require a different UX treatment.
  3. Completeness inference accuracy: How reliably can the system infer a specific meal from basket contents? If inference is weak, the context-aware completeness check will trigger too rarely or surface irrelevant suggestions, limiting the value of Step 4.
