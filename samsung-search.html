Samsung Electronics America · 2022–2024

Hybrid AI-Search
at Samsung.com

Improving on-site search relevance by selectively augmenting lexical retrieval with semantic understanding

AI/ML Search E-commerce
The Problem Solution Strategy Why This Approach Impact
The Problem

Samsung.com's on-site search was built primarily on lexical matching. That worked well for exact queries, but often broke down when intent was ambiguous. A search like "Galaxy Watch" could return a mix of phones, watches, and TVs because the engine matched shared tokens rather than actual shopping intent.

As the catalog expanded, the short-term fix was to layer on rules: category dictionaries, synonym maps, homonym handling, and redirect logic. Each rule addressed a specific failure case, but the system became increasingly brittle. Fixing one query could create regressions elsewhere, and maintaining relevance meant maintaining a growing set of exceptions that did not scale.

Solution Strategy

Rather than replacing the search stack entirely, I focused on identifying where lexical search was actually failing users and applying AI only to those cases.

Step 1 — Identify the failure surface

I used low search-result CTR as a proxy for intent mismatch. Queries with impressions but weak click engagement often indicated that results were technically related, but not aligned with what users actually meant. These queries became the target set for intervention.

Step 2 — Validate the concept

I ran an initial proof of concept using semantic embeddings (SBERT) to test whether vector retrieval could better resolve ambiguous, intent-heavy queries than lexical matching alone.

Step 3 — Design a hybrid routing model

I partnered with Samsung's AI Research team to implement a hybrid search flow: low-CTR, high-ambiguity queries were routed to an in-house vector search model, while exact-match queries continued through the existing lexical pipeline. This let us improve relevance where semantic understanding mattered most without disrupting high-performing keyword behavior.

Why This Approach

A full replacement would have introduced unnecessary risk. Lexical search still performed well for exact product queries like "Galaxy S24 256GB", where precision and determinism mattered most. The better product decision was not to replace search with AI everywhere, but to selectively augment the experience where users were most likely to encounter intent mismatch.

To reduce launch risk, I rolled the hybrid routing model out in phases — 5%, 20%, and then 50% of eligible query traffic — validating relevance and engagement performance at each stage before expanding exposure.

The result was a more scalable search strategy: preserving the speed and reliability of lexical search while using AI to improve the subset of queries where rules were no longer enough.

Impact

I evaluated the rollout across three layers: Precision@3 to measure top-result relevance, search-result CTR to capture whether users engaged with the returned results, and search conversion to determine whether improved relevance translated into downstream business impact. This ensured the model was assessed not only on retrieval quality, but on whether it actually improved the shopping experience.
