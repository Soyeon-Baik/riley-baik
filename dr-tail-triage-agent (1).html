Designing a Real-Time AI Triage Agent for Pet Health

How I would replace a rule-based emergency triage with a real-time AI agent that delivers instant, context-aware action plans.

Overview

Move from static triage questions to a real-time AI assistant that classifies urgency and escalates safely.

Problem: Static questionnaires can slow urgency recognition when pet owners need fast guidance.

Solution: A real-time AI assistant that assesses urgency and provides context-aware next-step guidance.

AI role: LLM-based urgency classification with safety guardrails, confidence thresholds, and escalation logic.

PM focus: Build trust through responsible AI rollout, clear escalation paths, and a robust evaluation framework.

Introduction

Dr. Tail positions itself as a 24/7 AI vet platform, but its core triage experience still breaks at the moment users need help most. Instead of delivering immediate guidance, it relies on a rule-based questionnaire and asks users to wait for a human response.

That gap is more than a UX issue. In high-stress health situations, delay creates both user frustration and safety risk.

This case study explores how I would redesign that flow as a Real-Time AI Triage Agent — one that classifies urgency, delivers context-aware next steps, and is designed with safety, evaluation, and rollout constraints from the start.

The Problem

The existing triage flow identifies risk but stops short of actionable guidance.

Dr. Tail's current triage flow helps identify that something may be wrong, but it stops short at the moment users need the most help. It does not translate urgency into immediate next-step guidance, and it does not interpret symptoms deeply enough in the context of the specific pet. As a result, owners are left waiting in high-stress moments without clear, personalized direction until a veterinarian reviews the case.

2 AM scenario. A cat owner notices their cat vomiting repeatedly. They open Dr. Tail, answer four questions, and see: "Serious Concern (Level 7). Waiting for Dr. Tail Vet's answer..." — with no guidance on what to do while they wait.
Current experience
Problem 1 · No immediate actionability

The current experience identifies possible risk, but it does not help users act on it. In urgent situations, owners need immediate guidance on whether to go to the ER, seek urgent care, or monitor safely at home.

Problem 2 · Limited contextual interpretation

The same symptom can signal very different levels of urgency depending on the pet's age, breed, history, and co-occurring symptoms. A rule-based flow can capture obvious cases, but it struggles when severity depends on multiple signals that need to be interpreted together.

AI Problem Framing

Two sequential AI tasks: classify urgency, then generate an action plan.

This problem is a strong fit for AI because the product needs to do two things in sequence: interpret urgency from incomplete, context-dependent signals, then turn that assessment into clear next-step guidance.

Task 1 — Urgency Classification

Classify the pet's condition into one of four urgency tiers based on the owner's current symptom report (including at least one multimodal input: text, image, or video) and the pet's existing health profile (breed, age, medical history).

Task 2 — Action Protocol Generation

Once urgency is classified, generate a context-aware action plan: a short plain-language assessment, 2–3 immediate care steps, and a recommended next action. This is best handled as a grounded generation task using veterinary guidance and the pet's health history.

Data Strategy

To make these two AI tasks reliable in practice, the system needs a data strategy that supports strong supervision, broad input coverage, and continuous improvement over time. Three sources work together:

  • Cold-start support data: Public and synthetic examples to expand coverage for owner-language symptom descriptions and rare scenarios.
  • Internal clinical data: Historical cases connecting initial symptom reports to veterinarian-reviewed outcomes — the strongest supervision signal.
  • Ongoing feedback loop: User actions and follow-up outcomes to improve the model over time, especially for edge cases and calibration.

Labeling relies primarily on vet-reviewed clinical outcomes. For cases where outcomes are ambiguous or clinical stakes are high, expert audits add a validation layer before those examples are used for training.

Architecture

A fine-tuned SLM chosen for clinical accuracy, latency, and privacy.

For a safety-sensitive triage experience, a fine-tuned Small Language Model (SLM) was the better choice over a general-purpose LLM for three reasons.

Clinical accuracy

Fine-tuning on vet-validated EMR data creates a domain specialist. A generic LLM is broader, but more likely to produce plausible-sounding guidance that is not clinically reliable.

Latency + privacy

Emergency triage requires P95 < 3s response times. A lightweight SLM running on private cloud infrastructure meets that bar without external API calls, giving stronger control over health data privacy and compliance.

Trust & Guardrails

Trust is designed across three layers: behavior, uncertainty visibility, and continuous improvement.

Because this product influences real health decisions for pets, trust and guardrails are core to the product — not a post-launch concern.

Behavior Design

  • Structured output format. Every response follows a fixed structure: a one-sentence severity assessment, 2–3 immediate care steps, and a single next-step directive. This keeps outputs scannable under stress and prevents the model from drifting into open-ended explanations that delay action.
  • No diagnosis, no dosage. The agent never provides a definitive diagnosis or medication recommendation. All outputs are framed as triage assessments, not clinical conclusions.
  • Calm, direct tone. The agent is warm but never vague — "here's what to do now" rather than "it might be okay."
  • Auto-escalation rules. For known life-threatening symptom patterns (seizures, unresponsiveness, heavy bleeding), the agent skips full assessment and immediately outputs Sev 4 with the nearest ER via GPS.

Confidence-Aware UI

Rather than hiding uncertainty, the UI adapts to it:

Continuous Improvement

  • 48-hour follow-up survey. A push notification asks owners how their pet is doing and whether the guidance was helpful. Responses flagged as "Not helpful" are prioritized for vet review and the next retraining cycle.
  • Report an Issue button. A one-tap correction below every action protocol lets users flag if the urgency felt too high or too low. This disagreement signal feeds directly into the expert audit pipeline.
  • Subgroup fairness checks. The model is evaluated separately across breed types and age cohorts in every evaluation cycle. Any subgroup accuracy deviation above 5% triggers targeted retraining.

Experimentation

Offline evaluation → shadow mode → A/B, with an automatic kill switch.

The validation strategy mirrors the offline → shadow mode → A/B approach I've applied in production search systems. The key difference here: a guardrail failure in a medical context requires an automatic kill switch, not just a pause-and-review.

Phase 1 — Offline Evaluation

The fine-tuned SLM is evaluated against a Golden Set: historical cases where symptom inputs are paired with confirmed EMR diagnoses. Pass criteria: Recall (Sev 3–4) ≥ 95%, Precision ≥ 80%. The generation layer is scored by a veterinarian panel on medical accuracy (1–5 scale; target mean ≥ 4.0). This phase catches critical failures before the model touches any real user.

Phase 2 — Shadow Mode

The AI runs in production alongside the existing rule-based system, but outputs are hidden from users. Every symptom submission generates both the old score and the new AI triage. Key metric: Prediction Parity — disagreement rate between systems. Disagreement on urgency in >15% of cases routes to vet review. Shadow mode also stress-tests latency under real load to validate the P95 < 3s target. Duration: 4 weeks minimum, 1,000+ triage sessions.

Drift Monitoring & Kill Switch

The most realistic production threat is a new zoonotic disease outbreak, triggering both data drift (novel symptom descriptions) and concept drift (previously Sev 2 symptoms now requiring Sev 3 urgency). Three automated signals form an early-warning system: input distribution monitoring (embedding stability index), Escalation Accuracy Rate on a rolling 30-day window, and a spike in "Not Helpful" feedback responses (>15% weekly, up from ~5% baseline).

The AI Triage Agent automatically reverts to the rule-based baseline if: Recall (Sev 3–4) drops below 90% on a rolling 7-day window, hallucination rate exceeds 2%, or two or more confirmed Sev 4 misclassifications occur within any 7-day period. Affected users receive a push notification to consult a vet; the engineering team runs root-cause analysis before redeployment.

Why This Can Win

A closed-loop learning system that compounds over time.

Dr. Tail's EMR integration creates a closed-loop learning system that becomes stronger over time. Each triage recommendation can eventually be compared against veterinarian-confirmed outcomes from connected clinics, creating a growing source of labeled ground truth for model improvement.

Over time, the platform also becomes more valuable to pet owners as it accumulates each pet's longitudinal health history. That history improves personalization, while also raising switching costs: moving to another product would mean losing years of medical context. Together, these dynamics create a defensible advantage built on proprietary data and feedback loops, not just surface-level features.
