How I would replace a rule-based emergency triage with an AI agent that delivers instant, context-aware action plans — and why this can win with competitive moat

Introduction

Dr. Tail positions itself as a 24/7 AI vet platform, but its core triage experience still breaks at the moment users need help most. Instead of delivering immediate guidance, it relies on a rule-based questionnaire and asks users to wait for a human response.

That gap is more than a UX issue. In high-stress health situations, delay creates both user frustration and safety risk.

This case study explores how I would redesign that flow as a Real-Time AI Triage Agent — one that classifies urgency, delivers context-aware next steps, and is designed with safety, evaluation, and rollout constraints from the start.

The Problem

It's 2 AM. A cat owner notices their cat vomiting repeatedly. They open Dr. Tail, answer four questions, and see: "Serious Concern (Level 7). Waiting for Dr. Tail Vet's answer..." — with no guidance on what to do while they wait.

Dr. Tail's current triage flow helps identify that something may be wrong, but it stops short at the moment users need the most help. It does not translate urgency into immediate next-step guidance, and it does not interpret symptoms deeply enough in the context of the specific pet. As a result, owners are left waiting in high-stress moments without clear, personalized direction until a veterinarian reviews the case — often only to recommend an in-person visit anyway.

Problem 1 - No Immediate Actionability

The current experience identifies possible risk, but it does not help users act on it. In urgent situations, owners need immediate guidance on whether to go to the ER, seek urgent care, or monitor safely at home.

Problem 2 - Limited Contextual Interpretation

The same symptom can signal very different levels of urgency depending on the pet's age, breed, history, and co-occurring symptoms. A rule-based flow can capture obvious cases, but it struggles when severity depends on multiple signals that need to be interpreted together.

AI Problem Framing

This problem is a strong fit for AI because the product needs to do two things in sequence: interpret urgency from incomplete, context-dependent signals, then turn that assessment into clear next-step guidance. This breaks the product problem into two AI tasks: urgency classification and action protocol generation.

Task 1 — Urgency Classification

The first task is to classify the pet’s condition into one of four urgency tiers based on two inputs: the owner’s current symptom report, which may include at least one multimodal input such as text, image, or video, and the pet’s existing health profile already stored in the system, including breed, age, and medical history.

Task 2 — Action Protocol Generation

Once urgency is classified, the second task is to generate a context-aware action plan: a short plain-language assessment, 2–3 immediate care steps, and a recommended next action. This is best handled as a grounded generation task using veterinary guidance and the pet’s health history.

Data Strategy

To make these two AI tasks reliable in practice, the system needs a data strategy that supports strong supervision, broad input coverage, and continuous improvement over time. The following three sources work together to cover these needs:

  • Cold-start support data: Public and synthetic examples can help expand coverage for owner-language symptom descriptions and rare scenarios.
  • Internal clinical data: Historical cases that connect initial symptom reports to veterinarian-reviewed outcomes provide the strongest supervision signal.
  • Ongoing feedback loop: User actions and follow-up outcomes can help improve the model over time, especially for edge cases and calibration.

Labeling relies primarily on vet-reviewed clinical outcomes, where a confirmed diagnosis is linked back to the original symptom input to create a ground-truth label. For cases where outcomes are ambiguous or clinical stakes are high, expert audits add an extra validation layer before those examples are used for training.

Architecture

Model Strategy: Fine-Tuned SLM

For a safety-sensitive triage experience like this, a fine-tuned Small Language Model (SLM) was the better choice for three reasons.

1. Clinical accuracy

Fine-tuning on vet-validated EMR data creates a domain specialist. A generic LLM is broader, but more likely to produce plausible-sounding guidance that is not clinically reliable.

2. Latency

Emergency triage requires fast response times, with a target of P95 < 3 seconds. A lightweight SLM is more likely to meet that bar consistently in production.

3. Data privacy

Pet health records and symptom images contain sensitive personal data. Running inference on private cloud infrastructure without external API calls gives the product stronger control over privacy, security, and compliance.

Trust & Guardrails

Because this product influences real health decisions for pets, trust and guardrails are core to the product. In practice, that trust has to be designed across three layers: the AI’s behavior, the mechanisms that make uncertainty and risk visible to users, and the feedback loops that help the system improve over time.

Behavior Design

Behavior was designed at the product level first, then enforced through prompts and system constraints.

  • Structured output format. Every response follows a fixed structure: a one-sentence severity assessment, 2–3 immediate care steps, and a single next-step directive. This keeps outputs scannable under stress and prevents the model from drifting into open-ended explanations that delay action.
  • No diagnosis, no dosage. The agent never provides a definitive diagnosis or medication recommendation. All outputs are framed as triage assessments, not clinical conclusions.
  • Calm, direct tone. The agent is warm but never vague, prioritizing clarity over comfort — "here's what to do now" rather than "it might be okay."
  • Auto-escalation rules. For known life-threatening symptom patterns such as seizures, unresponsiveness, or heavy bleeding, the agent skips full assessment and immediately outputs Sev 4 with the nearest ER via GPS.

Trust Mechanisms

Trust also depends on how the system handles failure, privacy, and uncertainty at runtime.

  • Top risks and mitigations. The highest-stakes risks were false negatives on life-threatening cases, data leakage from health records and photos, and over-escalation fatigue that could weaken trust over time. Each risk was paired with a clear mitigation: vet-validated training data and critical-symptom watchlists, private-cloud inference with ephemeral photo handling, and escalation accuracy monitoring tied to retraining thresholds.
  • Confidence-aware UI. Rather than hiding uncertainty, the UI adapts to it. High confidence (≥85%) surfaces the full protocol. Moderate confidence (60–84%) adds a dotted badge and an option to provide more detail. Low confidence (<60%) routes directly to a human vet without showing uncertain output to the user.
  • Subgroup fairness checks. The model is evaluated separately across breed types and age cohorts in every evaluation cycle. Any subgroup accuracy deviation above 5% triggers targeted retraining.

Continuous Improvement

Because no triage model is perfect at launch, the product was designed to learn continuously from disagreement and real-world outcomes.

  • 48-hour follow-up survey. A push notification asks owners how their pet is doing and whether the guidance was helpful. Responses such as "Not helpful — it was more/less serious" are flagged for veterinarian review and prioritized in the next retraining cycle.
  • Report an Issue button. A one-tap correction below every action protocol lets users flag if the urgency felt too high or too low. This disagreement signal feeds directly into the expert audit pipeline.
  • Expert audit and retraining loop. Flagged cases from both mechanisms are reviewed by veterinarians, re-labeled where needed, and used to retrain the model — closing the loop between real-world outcomes and model behavior.

Experimentation Plan

The validation strategy mirrors the offline → shadow mode → A/B approach I've applied in production search systems — the key difference here being the safety stakes: a guardrail failure in a medical context requires an automatic kill switch, not just a pause-and-review.

Phase 1 — Offline Evaluation

The fine-tuned SLM is evaluated against a Golden Set: historical cases where symptom inputs are paired with confirmed EMR diagnoses. Pass criteria: Recall (Sev 3–4) ≥ 95%, Precision ≥ 80%. The generation layer is scored by a veterinarian panel on medical accuracy (1–5 scale; target mean ≥ 4.0). This phase catches critical failures before the model touches any real user.

Phase 2 — Shadow Mode

The AI runs in production alongside the existing rule-based system, but outputs are hidden from users. Every symptom submission generates both the old score and the new AI triage. Key metric: Prediction Parity — disagreement rate between systems. Disagreement on urgency in >15% of cases routes to vet review. Shadow mode also stress-tests latency under real load to validate the P95 < 3s target.

Duration: 4 weeks minimum, 1,000+ triage sessions.

Drift Monitoring & Kill Switch

The most realistic production threat is a new zoonotic disease outbreak — triggering both data drift (novel symptom descriptions) and concept drift (previously Sev 2 symptoms now requiring Sev 3 urgency). Three automated signals form an early-warning system: input distribution monitoring (embedding stability index), Escalation Accuracy Rate on a rolling 30-day window, and a spike in "Not Helpful" feedback responses (>15% weekly, up from ~5% baseline).

The AI Triage Agent automatically reverts to the rule-based baseline if: Recall (Sev 3–4) drops below 90% on a rolling 7-day window, hallucination rate exceeds 2%, or two or more confirmed Sev 4 misclassifications occur within any 7-day period. Affected users receive a push notification to consult a vet; the engineering team runs root-cause analysis before redeployment.

Why This Can Win

Dr. Tail’s EMR integration creates a closed-loop learning system that becomes stronger over time. Each triage recommendation can eventually be compared against veterinarian-confirmed outcomes from connected clinics, creating a growing source of labeled ground truth for model improvement.

Over time, the platform also becomes more valuable to pet owners as it accumulates each pet’s longitudinal health history. That history improves personalization, while also raising switching costs: moving to another product would mean losing years of medical context. Together, these dynamics create a defensible advantage built on proprietary data and feedback loops, not just surface-level features.
